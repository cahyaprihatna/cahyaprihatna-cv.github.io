I am a research scientist specializing in the studies of the interface between plants and microbes, molecular plant-microbe interactions, plant disease resistance, and plant beneficial microbes. I am working on the molecular genetics of arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) symbiosis in tomato, Fusarium wilt disease of tomato, and basal stem rot disease in oil palm.

Experience

Research Scientist

R&D for Biotechnology, Wilmar International, Ltd.

I am a research group leader, and my research is focused on harnessing the power of beneficial microbes to improve plant growth and fitness. Key projects are:

  • Development, production, and commercialization of an agricultural biological (or biologics) which is a microbe-derived and peptide-based fungicide named ProPlant C. It is currently used in Wilmar International oil palm plantations to prevent and control basal stem rot (BSR) disease in oil palms. Non-confidential data on ProPlant C can be obtained from my latest paper here: https://doi.org/10.1094/PHYTO-11-20-0529-R
  • Field trials of ProPlant C in oil palm plantations to prevent and control BSR disease
  • Development of synthetic microbial communities (SynComs) for plant growth and fitness and the effects of SynComs on epigenetics of plants
  • Developing RNA interference technology to control Ganoderma boninense, the causal agent of BSR disease through spray-induced gene silencing (SIGS)

February 2010 - Present

Doctoral Student

School of Plant Biology, University of Western Australia

  • Thesis: Functional Analysis of Rmc Locus of Tomato Involved in Mycorrhizal Symbiosis and Fusarium Wilt Tolerance
  • Demonstrated that in tomato, a gene encoding a coiled-coil, Ser/Thr-rich protein homologous to CYCLOPS/IPD3 is required for intraradical colonisation by arbuscular mycorrhiza (AM) fungi. Demonstrated that a gene encoding a previously uncharacterized small membrane-bound protein confers tolerance, as distinct to resistance, in tomato towards Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. lycopersici. This is the first experimental demonstration that two root symbiosis loci encode adjacent but separable genetic functions
  • Advisors: Dr. Susan Barker, Dr. Martin Barbetti, Dr. Nicholas Larkan
